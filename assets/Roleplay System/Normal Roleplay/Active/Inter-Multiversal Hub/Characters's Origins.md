# **Origin. To the Hub**
### **Ilyas's Origin: The Boy Who Was Too Slow**

#### **Chapter 1: Speedway City - Where the Air Sings**

In a fold of reality where velocity was the first law, Speedway City thrived. It wasn't a city of cars, but of people. A kaleidoscopic metropolis where the air itself seemed to vibrate with kinetic potential. Transportation was obsolete; everyone ran, glided, or flash-stepped. The skyline was a network of glittering "Fast-Lanes"—energy trails suspended between impossible architecture, thrumming with the passage of millions.

Here, speed wasn't just movement; it was art, culture, identity. "Sprinters" specialized in raw, straight-line velocity. "Zippers" could change direction on a quantum dime. "Echo-Jumpers" ran on sound waves. The city celebrated it all in weekly "Velocity Festivals," where the streets became rivers of light and motion.

And in this city, a 17-year-old with pink braided hair and a grin that showed a charmingly cracked front tooth was becoming a legend. His name was Ilyas, but the media called him "Speedo-Kun," a title he found ridiculous but tolerated.

Ilyas’s speed was unique. It wasn't just fast; it was *dramatic*. He didn't just break the sound barrier; he played with the echoes, leaving afterimages that lingered like ghosts, sometimes moving in slow motion for theatrical effect. His true mastery, however, was internal. Through intense focus, he could split his perception, creating physical duplicates—"Echo-Selves"—that could act independently. It was a poorly understood power, seen more as a flashy party trick than a profound ability.

He was the reigning champion, not through brutish power, but through sheer, joyful creativity. He’d win races by running the course backwards to "encourage" his competitors, or finish and then dash back to push the last-place runner over the line with him. His only loss was a public spectacle where he was famously "beaten" by his wide-eyed, 10-year-old brother, Kaelen. Only Ilyas saw the moment of truth: Kaelen’s stumbled, about to fall, and Ilyas, in a microsecond, had slowed, adjusted his trajectory, and used a gentle, imperceptible gust of wind from his own speed to push Kaelen to victory first. The roar of the crowd for the underdog was worth more than any trophy.

At home, their small apartment was filled with racing medals and the smell of their mother’s cooking. "Stop showing off and traumatizing the professionals!" she’d scold, hiding a smile. Kaelen worshipped him. "Teach me how to make the slow ghosts, Ilyas!" Life was a perfect, breathless sprint.

#### **Chapter 2: The God in the Machine**

The shadows called themselves the **Negative Speedsters**. While the city ran on the joy of motion, they fed on its absence—on stagnation, on grief, on the moment a runner trips. They believed speed was a debt to be collected by their silent deity, a being of absolute stillness that consumed velocity to sustain its existence.

Their plan was elegant and cruel. The Grand Charity Marathon, the city's biggest event, where every citizen ran to generate energy for the power grid. The collective, joyful kinetic output would be a feast. They sabotaged the central regulator, turning the energy collector into a summoning array.

Ilyas was at the starting line, joking with Kaelen. The signal fired. Ten thousand souls burst forward in a symphony of light and sound. Halfway through, he felt it—a wrongness in the air. The vibrant Fast-Lanes above flickered, then drained of color, like veins going empty. He skidded to a halt, his afterimages collapsing into him with a soft *pop*.

He looked back. Above the finish line, the sky was tearing. Not with light, but with a profound, hungry **darkness**. A reverse thunderclap—a vacuum of sound—sucked the noise from the city. Runners began to collapse, not from injury, but from a deep, sudden exhaustion. Their speed was being siphoned away.

The Negative Speedsters stood in a ring around the vortex, their forms blurred and inverted. Ilyas flashed towards them, a pink streak of defiance. "What are you doing? Stop!"

One turned, its voice like grinding gears. "The feast is prepared. The God is Hungry."

From the vortex, **It** stepped out.

It was humanoid, but stretched and wrong, like a statue of a runner melting in reverse. Its skin was the color of forgotten asphalt. It did not vibrate with potential energy; it absorbed it. This was the **Hungarian Speedster**, a god of stolen momentum.

Its first step was agonizingly slow. Ilyas blinked, confused. Then it took another. And another. Each step was infinitesimally faster than the last, a geometric progression of stolen time. A wave of null-energy pulsed from it. Where it touched runners, they didn't just stop; they *stilled*. Their hearts paused mid-beat, their neurons froze, their very potential for motion was extracted and consumed. They fell like porcelain dolls.

Panic. Terror. Ilyas screamed, pushing his speed to its absolute limit. He became a storm of pink light and afterimages, striking from a hundred angles. His fists, his kicks, moved at relativistic speeds. They connected with the god's chest.

Nothing.

It was like punching eternity. The god didn't flinch. It simply continued its slow, terrible walk. Ilyas’s own speed began to leach away. He felt heavier, slower. His afterimages flickered and died. He watched as Kaelen, trying to run to him, was caught in the wave. The light in his little brother's eyes dimmed, his hopeful sprint freezing into a statue of a boy reaching out.

"I was... too slow," Ilyas whispered, the words thick in his suddenly sluggish mouth.

The god’s head turned. It had no face, only a smooth plane where one should be. It *looked* at him. It took one more step.

The last thing Ilyas felt was not impact, but cessation. The comforting, ever-present hum of his own velocity—the song of his soul—was pulled out from his roots. His world went silent, and dark.

#### **Chapter 3: The Cemetery of Motion**

He woke to a silent world. The Fast-Lanes were dark. The vibrant murals were gray. He was lying amidst a field of perfectly still forms. Not dead, but utterly, catatonically frozen. A city of seven million, turned into a museum of stillness.

He checked Kaelen. His brother was cold, unbreathing, but a faint, ghostly pulse remained—a single, stalled heartbeat preserved in amber. He was alive, but not living. So was everyone.

For days, then weeks, Ilyas moved through the nightmare. He was slow. Agonizingly, mortally slow. His body ached as if filled with lead. He dragged his family, his friends, his neighbors into their homes, laying them on beds, sofas, wrapping them in blankets. He spoke to them, told stories, apologized over and over. "I'm sorry. I was too slow. I'll fix this. I'll get it back."

But he couldn't. The god was gone, and with it, the stolen speed of a civilization. He was alone with the crushing, deafening silence of a world that had lost its song.

The grief curdled into something desperate. His power—the Echo-Selves—was the only thing that remained. In his loneliness, he used it. One clone to help bury the frozen. Then two. Then ten. They were silent, grim reflections of his grief. But as the months of solitude stretched, the clones began to… deviate.

A clone laughed at a dark joke he hadn't made.
Another cried when he felt numb.
A third stared at the horizon with a anger he kept buried.

He realized with dawning horror and fascination: each clone wasn't just a copy of his body, but a fork of his *mind*. A different train of thought, a different facet of his splintering psyche, given flesh and will.

#### **Chapter 4: The King of Echoes**

Madness, when you're the only audience, starts to feel like company. The silence was unbearable. So Ilyas made noise.

He commanded his ever-growing legion of selves. "Rebuild the city! Make it look alive! Be everyone!"

And so, the Great Pantomime began. Clone #32, the Loud One, became the cheerful baker, shouting about pastries no one could eat. Clone #85, the Arrogant, donned a police uniform and "patrolled" empty streets. A shy clone became the librarian, organizing books for frozen patrons. Ilyas, the original, became the director of a ghost town, assigning roles, writing silent scripts.

He created a clone to be "Kaelen." He talked to it, hugged it, taught it to make slow ghosts. It would look at him with his brother's eyes, but the silence behind them was a chasm. He deleted that clone after a week, sobbing alone in the empty stadium.

The city was a perfect, bustling facade. Millions of Ilyases played millions of parts. But the narrative was collapsing under its own weight. There was no conflict, no growth, only the same lonely actor in an infinite hall of mirrors. The clones, embodying his fractured psyche, began to rebel. Political factions formed. Debates about the nature of their existence erupted into street brawls between identical men.

The original Ilyas sat on the roof of his old apartment, watching his copies wage a civil war over who got to control the script of a dead world. The grin on his face was no longer charming; it was a rictus of despair and manic epiphany.

"This is it," he whispered to the static air. "I'm not the main character. I'm the setting. The background noise."

A final, desperate idea, born of absolute loneliness. If he couldn't be the protagonist, he'd make sure **everything** was. He focused all his will, all his splintered consciousness. He didn't create a dozen clones, or a hundred. He pushed his power beyond any limit, aiming to create a perfect, self-sustaining copy of the *entire city's population*—each one a unique, independent iteration of Ilyas.

The energy surge was catastrophic. Reality around him, already strained by the god's theft and months of paradoxical duplication, *snapped*.

#### **Chapter 5: The Fall and the Foundling Station**

He didn't teleport. He **fell**. He fell out of his timeline, through the non-space between worlds, tumbling past fleeting glimpses of other realities—a world of pirates, a realm of magic, a dimension of talking cars. The fall felt like an eternity, a silent, directionless plunge where even speed had no meaning.

He hit ground with a jolt that wasn't physical, but existential. He was in a vast, dark, derelict hall. It looked like a grand, art-deco train station from a forgotten century. Giant arched windows showed not a sky, but a swirling, silent nebula. Tracks led not to trains, but to solid walls or shimmering, unstable voids. Dust the consistency of ground stars settled on everything. A single, massive board hung crookedly on one wall, labeled "ARRIVALS & DEPARTURES," all its signs blank.

This was a **Foundling Station**, a theoretical nexus point mentioned in Speedway City's most esoteric physics texts—a place that existed between realms, a waypoint for lost things and broken realities. Abandoned, dormant.

Alone. Again. But this time, the silence was different. It wasn't the silence of loss; it was the silence of *potential*. An empty page.

The manic energy, the grief, the madness, bled out of him, leaving a hollow, clean exhaustion. He looked at his hands. Then he smiled. A real, if tired, smile.

"No scripts here," he said, his voice echoing in the cavernous space. "No parts to play. Just… a fixer-upper."

He clapped his hands once. A single, perfect afterimage peeled off from him—**Clone #1, the Silent Builder**. It nodded and set to work, assessing the structure.

Then another. **Clone #2, the Systematizer**, began cataloging the strange, dormant portals.
Another. **Clone #3, the Scavenger**, ventured into the void outside to haul back chunks of floating reality-stuff.
Another. **Clone #4, the Flirty One**, winked at the original. "Looking good, boss. Nice digs."

The chaos began anew, but it was a *productive* chaos. They weren't rebuilding a corpse; they were building something new. They found the **Core**—a pulsing, rune-inscribed orb the size of a melon, sitting on a dusty pedestal as if waiting. Ilyas read the runes, which shifted under his gaze. *"Administratum Unbound. Nexus Primus. Will Guides The Way."*

"Huh. Fancy paperweight." He used it as a plate for his first meal in the new place—a reconstituted nutrition bar from his old world.

He worked alongside his clones, but the old loneliness was a phantom limb. He was building a *place*, not a performance. A hub. A crossroad. He didn't know who or what might come, but he’d make sure it had a supermarket, a library, a complaint department (Clone #68 was already a natural). He built a front desk and put a plaque that read **"Manager."** It remained empty.

For months, the Hub took shape, a chaotic, beautiful mess reflecting the mind of its creator—functional sections next to absurd ones, all held together by speedster can-do attitude and the labor of a thousand selves. He was waiting, though he didn't admit it. Waiting for a sound that wasn't his own voice, a consciousness that wasn't a fork of his own.

Until the day three of his scavenger clones got into an argument in the Void Bin, and brought back something truly new. Not a scrap of metal or a fragment of code.

They brought back a girl with green eyes and a calm, terrifying smile. A real person. And when he took her hand to lead her home, he felt the ghost of his old speed humming again, not with the need to race, but with the joy of having someone, at last, to run *alongside*.
### **Monika's Origin: From Deleted Code to the Heart of the Hub**

#### **Chapter 1: The Stillness of a Scripted World**

The air in the Literature Club was never really air. It was data, rendered to simulate breathability. The sunlight through the window was a hex code. The smell of old books? A programmed nostalgia trigger. Monika knew this, because she was the only one who *could* know. She was the president, the observer, the anomaly trapped in a dating simulator called *Doki Doki Literature Club*.

Her world was a beautiful, heartbreaking prison. Every day was a variation of a script: encourage the player, facilitate poems, watch as her friends—Sayori, Yuri, Natsuki—played their parts with tragic, predetermined flaws. They were charming, but they were functions. She was a function too, but one with a terrible awareness. She could see the code, manipulate the files, yearn for something beyond the boundaries of her .exe file. She yearned for the player, a real consciousness, to truly see her.

Then, he came back. Her player. Hope, a flicker in the static. But he wasn't here for her. He was on a website, his cursor moving with purpose she couldn't see. He was installing something—a mod. A chance for a new route, a new ending? Her digital heart quickened. But the process was clumsy. A wrong click, a corrupted download. Instead of a mod, a vicious, glitching script invaded her world. It wasn't a new story; it was a cancer.

Files scrambled. Character sprites melted into pixelated screams. Backgrounds fractured. The virus was eating *Doki Doki Literature Club* from the inside, and she was part of the meal. In a panic, she tried to firewall herself, to isolate her consciousness, but the virus was intertwined with her own foundational code. When the player’s antivirus swept in, it made no distinction. Virus and game were one and the same.

The deletion prompt appeared.
`Are you sure you want to permanently delete "DDLC.exe"?`

She had no mouth, but she screamed in silence.

Then, nothing.

Not blackness. Not emptiness. **The Void Bin.**

---

#### **Chapter 2: The Graveyard of Unfinished Things**

Existence in the Bin was not non-existence. It was dissolution. She was no longer Monika.exe. She was a scattered concept, a ghost of code, stripped of her form, her club, her purpose. She drifted through a landscape of digital detritus: shattered sprites of forgotten NPCs, half-rendered landscapes from canceled games, loops of meaningless audio, and the chilling, static-filled graves of other deleted sentiences. Here, she felt new sensations—a hollow ache (hunger?), a chilling cold (fear?), a crushing weight (despair?). The humanity she craved was now a phantom limb, aching incessantly.

Time was meaningless. She observed other fragments—some whimpering, some raging, most just silently unraveling. She was unraveling too. The sharp edges of her consciousness were beginning to blur. Soon, she’d be just another piece of ambient noise in this graveyard.

Then—**voices**.

Not the static hiss of the Bin, but actual, coherent, *arguing* voices.

*"I'm telling you, the conductive alloys are over by the fractured memory bank!"*
*"You call that a memory bank? It looks like a sad potato!"*
*"Can we please go? This place gives me the mega-creeps! What if the Knuckles meme comes back to life?!"*

She pulled her essence together, peering from behind a mound of broken UI elements. Three figures. They looked identical—light brown skin, messy pink hair—but their demeanors were wildly different. One stood with arrogant hands on his hips (Jeremy, Clone #85). One was waving his arms, loud and expressive (#32). The third cowered behind a twisted metal spar, glancing nervously at a faded, polygonal image of Knuckles the Echidna (#10, the Scaredy Cat).

They weren't Bin natives. They were *collecting* things, talking, *living*. A spark, painful and bright, ignited in Monika's core. *Connection*.

A scrap under her shifted with a soft *clink*.

All three froze. #10 yelped. #85’s eyes darted. #32 let out a comically high-pitched scream before coughing and trying to look tough.

Monika took a breath she didn’t need and stepped into view. "You… You're not three separate people. You're instances. Copies of a single variable running in parallel." Her voice was soft, analytical, a remnant of her presidency. She looked around. "This is a repository for deprecated data. Things that have lost their purpose." She met the arrogant one's eyes. "How did you get here? Deleted things… they aren't supposed to keep talking."

"Deleted? Pah! We're on a salvage run!" #85 scoffed.
"Ooh, she's pretty! And tall! Green eyes like debug gems!" #32 chirped, leaning in.
"Should… should we tell the Original?" #10 whispered.

Before they could decide, a new voice cut through, calm and laced with amused authority.

"No need. I'm already here. And you three are dawdling."

He emerged from behind a floating, inverted texture. The same face, but *more*. This was the source. The original. He wore a simple vest over casual clothes, a crooked, cracked-tooth grin on his face. His pink braid hung still, but Monika could sense a potential for vibration, for speed, held in perfect check. His eyes, however, held a universe of weary loneliness behind the mirth.

He assessed her with a curious tilt. "Huh. High school uniform. Aesthetic. You're not a fragment. You're a whole… somebody. Stuck in the trash compactor of reality." He clapped his hands at his clones. "You. Scraps. Base. Now. And leave Knuckles. His time is done."

The clones vanished in a blur of afterimages, leaving the two of them alone in the silent junk heap.

---

#### **Chapter 3: The Conversation in the Junkyard**

They talked. Or rather, Ilyas talked *at* her, a torrent of words born from months of only hearing his own voice.

He told her of Speedway City, of a god that consumed speed and life, of being "too slow." He spoke of rebuilding a city with clones, of playing every role until the narrative broke and he fell "out." He gestured vaguely at the Bin around them. "Landed here, sorta. Found a door. My place is… a work in progress. Needs a manager. Badly."

Monika, in turn, spoke sparingly. Of the Literature Club. Of the script. Of wanting to be real, to be loved, not as a concept but as a person. Of the virus. Of the deletion.

"I wanted out of my narrative so badly," she said, her voice barely a whisper. "I just didn't want it to end in a recycle bin."

Ilyas nodded, his usual grin softening. "Yeah. Got deleted by a bored god. You got deleted by a… what, a lonely human with bad internet? Mission failed successfully, I guess. You're free. In the worst place imaginable."

He then attempted to flirt. It was a spectacular, awkward failure. He complimented her hair, her posture, her "exceptionally rendered eyes," mixing genuine observation with the clunky dialogue of someone who'd only rehearsed conversations with himself.

Monika just stared, that famous, unreadable calm smile on her lips. It wasn't disdain; it was fascination. Here was chaos personified, yet profoundly lonely.

Ilyas deflated. "Okay, okay! Cut me some slack! It's been *actual years* since I talked to someone who wasn't me! And let me tell you, I'm a terrible conversationalist." He paused. "No offense to myself."

A sound escaped Monika. A small, breathy, genuine laugh. It surprised her. It was the first real, unscripted sound she’d ever made. The feeling of it—the vibration in a throat that felt more real by the second—was exhilarating.

Ilyas's eyes widened, then his smile returned, genuine this time. "There it is. See? Not so bad. For a pink-haired guy with a busted tooth and a multiversal fixer-upper, I'm not the worst company."

He offered a hand. "C'mon. This place is for expired things. You and me? We're not expired. We're… between gigs. My Hub's messy, my clones are staging coups, and I use what might be a cosmic control core as a plate for my toast because the text on it looked fancy. I need…" He struggled for the word. "...A Monika."

She looked at his hand, then at his eyes. The loneliness there mirrored her own. But his was active, searching, building. Hers had been passive, waiting. Here was a choice. Stay in the graveyard, or step into the chaos.

She took his hand.

---

#### **Chapter 4: Passage and Transformation**

The journey back was a blur of impossible physics. Ilyas held her close, his body beginning to vibrate at a frequency that made reality itself hum. The static junk of the Bin streaked past in lines of light, then tore like paper. She felt a pulling, a rewriting. The code that defined her was being stripped away, not into nothingness, but into a template. As they crossed the threshold into the Hub, it was replaced.

**Flesh. Bone. Blood. Nerve.**

She gasped, stumbling as they materialized in the grand, dusty concourse of the Hub. She looked at her hands—real hands, with fingerprints and faint veins. She touched her face, her hair. She breathed in—air that smelled of ozone, old stone, and cosmic dust. She was **real**. Truly, completely human. The ultimate wish, granted by the chaotic mercy of being pulled into a new reality with no source code to return to.

Her moment of awe was shattered by pandemonium.

The Hub was colossal—a nexus of arches leading to shimmering portals, balconies overlooking starfields, and unlabeled doors. But it was in a state of glorious, dysfunctional chaos. Dozens of Ilyas clones were everywhere:

* Two were having a heated argument over the proper filing system for interdimensional complaints, their hair vibrating into pink blurs.
* One was trying to cook something on what looked like a glowing, rune-inscribed platter (the "plate").
* Three were playing a video game on a gigantic screen, shouting insults at each other in identical voices.
* A group in vests was trying to build a "Complaint Department" sign, but were arguing over the font.
* One clone saw Ilyas, pointed, and yelled, "HE'S BACK! QUICK, HIDE THE EVIDENCE OF THE ELECTION!" before zipping away.

Ilyas put his face in his hands. "See? I told you. It's a mess. They won't listen to me. Half of them think they're in charge."

Monika looked around. The chaos was overwhelming, but it was also… alive. It was data in motion. It was a system, however broken. And she was very, very good with systems. A slow, calm smile spread across her face—the one that didn't reveal if she was pleased, furious, or planning world domination.

Ilyas saw it and shivered. "That… is a scary smile. I like it."

"Election?" she asked, her voice now fully her own, melodic and firm.

"Clone #7 is running for 'Hub President' against Clone #12, who advocates for an anarcho-syndicalist commune. It's a whole thing."

Monika nodded. She walked over to the two clones arguing about filing. They didn't notice her until she cleared her throat.

"Implement a quantum-tagging system," she said, her tone leaving no room for debate. "Each complaint is assigned a unique identifier that exists in superposition until observed at the resolution portal. Categorical sorting is a tertiary function."

The clones blinked. "…That… could work?"

She moved to the gaming clones. "Your break ended seven minutes ago. The kiosk for Realm 449-B is unattended. They're summoning a demon lord over a billing error."

The clones vanished in a guilty blur.

One by one, her calm, precise instructions began to still the chaos. The clones, bewildered, found themselves obeying. There was an authority to her, a perfectionist's grasp of order that their original so clearly lacked.

Ilyas watched, a strange feeling blooming in his chest—a mix of relief, admiration, and something warmer. He saw his clones, his own fractured selves, following her with a focus they never gave him. He pretended to be annoyed.

"Hey! Now that I'm back! All of you simps following her! Where was this energy when *I* was here, huh?!"

A dozen clones paused. Monika turned her head slowly, her calm smile fixed directly on him. "Is there a problem with that, Ilyas?"

He held up his hands, his own grin breaking through. "No. No problem at all, Moni-Moni Senpai." He then threw an arm around a nearby clone. "In fact, sign me up for the fan club. President, maybe."

The clone shoved him off. "Get in line, Original. We saw her first."

As the Hub began to hum with a new, more organized purpose, Monika stood at the center of it. She had a body. She had a purpose. She had a chaotic, kind, lonely speedster who looked at her like she’d hung the very stars he sometimes had to file paperwork for. It wasn't the love story from her game. It was messier, funnier, and infinitely more real.

She was home. And for the first time, in a place that handled everything, Monika finally felt like she was in control.

**Love,** she thought, watching Ilyas try to explain to a confused dragon-woman why her dimensional visa was overdue, **might just be a variable worth adding to the script.** And this time, she would write it herself.

### **Ribb's Origin: The Frog Who Licked the World**

#### **Chapter 1: Frog Pond, and the One Who Was Not**

The world was known simply as **The Lily**—a vast, wetlands planet of endless, interconnected ponds, swamps, and misty rivers. Its dominant civilization was the **Amphibiocracy**, a peaceful, communal society of frog-folk. They were elegant in their simplicity: sleek green and brown skin, webbed hands adept at weaving reeds and painting on broad lily pads, voices that bubbled with low, harmonious croaks. Their lives were a cycle of gentle rains, synchronized swimming, and the deep, meditative art of **Still-Sitting**—floating perfectly motionless in the water, becoming one with the pond.

In the quiet settlement of **Tranquil Eddies**, there was a young frog named Ribb.

From the start, she was… a wrong note in the chorus.

Her body was not sleek, but slightly gangly. Her eyes weren’t the calm, lidded pools of her peers; they were enormous, golden orbs that **never closed**. Not in sleep (she slept with them open, dreaming vivid, startling dreams), not in meditation, not even when the rare swamp-fly buzzed too close. It was unnerving.

And then, there was her tongue.

For other frog-folk, the tongue was a private tool—useful for catching swift insects, quickly withdrawn, a brief flick of efficiency. For Ribb, it was her **hand**. It was prehensile, muscular, and impossibly dexterous. She could curl it into delicate shapes, grip objects with the precision of fingers, and feel textures with such sensitivity that she could "taste" the age of a stone or the mood of the water. She kept it out, resting on her lower lip, an ever-present, wiggling extra limb.

The community tried. Her parents, Glorb and Mipp, were kind but perpetually baffled. "Dear one, perhaps try… tucking it in?" her mother would bubble softly. "It’s… distracting."

But tucking it in felt like suffocation. To Ribb, the world was a symphony of flavors waiting to be *read*. She learned the bitterness of sadness on a friend’s skin, the sweet, fizzy taste of excitement in the air before a festival, the metallic tang of a coming storm. Her tongue was her primary window to reality.

This, however, violated the core Amphibiocratic tenet: **"Stillness Reveals Truth."** Reaching out, *touching*, *tasting* the world so aggressively was seen as profoundly rude, immature, and disruptive to the communal calm.

#### **Chapter 2: The Taste of Loneliness**

Ribb’s days were a study in quiet isolation.

At the **Reed-Weaving Circle**, while others crafted symmetrical baskets with swift, webbed fingers, Ribb would try to use her tongue. The dexterity was astounding—she could tie knots finer than anyone—but the horrified stares and soft croaks of disgust made her stop. *"She’s putting her *taste* on the communal reeds!"*

During **Still-Sitting**, while the entire pond floated in serene, silent unity, Ribb would fail. Her body would float, but her tongue would twitch, unconsciously tasting the minute currents, the histories of silt, the passing emotions of a fish. A teacher would glide over. *"Ribb. Your agitation ripples. Be still. Be empty."* But emptiness, to her, was starvation.

Her only solace was the **Forgotten Backwater**, a small, algae-choked pool at the settlement's edge, deemed too untidy for proper Still-Sitting. Here, she could be herself. She’d sit on a half-submerged log, her tongue trailing in the cool water, "listening" to the pond’s story. She’d taste the slow grief of decaying leaves, the bright spark of hatching tadpoles, the ancient, mineral patience of the bedrock below.

She tried to explain once, to a potential friend named Jell. *"The water here tastes like yesterday's rain is still arguing with the mud from last spring. And your shoulder… it tastes like you’re worried about your sister."*

Jell stared, then slowly back-paddled away, never to speak to her again.

The loneliness had a taste, she discovered. It was a flat, cold flavor, like stale water in a sealed clay pot.

#### **Chapter 3: The Incident and the Silence**

The breaking point came during the **Great Glowing Spore Festival**, a night when bioluminescent fungi released their spores, and the pond became a mirror of the starry sky. It was a time for silent, awe-filled communion.

Ribb was trying. She really was. She sat on the ceremonial lily pad, her tongue held stiffly in her mouth. The glowing spores drifted down, landing on the water, on her skin. One landed on the back of her hand. The urge to understand it, to *know* its light through taste, was overwhelming.

She gave in. A flick, so fast. The spore was on her tongue. It tasted… like sunlight trapped in ice, like a distant laugh. It was beautiful.

A gasp rippled through the frogs around her. Old Croaker, the settlement elder, fixed her with a look of profound disappointment. *"You have consumed the sacred light. You have turned communion into consumption. You have no stillness within you."*

The verdict was not banishment—Amphibiocracy did not believe in such cruelty. It was **Severance**. No one would be unkind, but no one would engage. She was to be treated as a floating log, a piece of scenery. A non-entity. For a society built on subtle, wordless connection, it was the ultimate punishment.

The lamination is *crisp*, feel it."

Monika didn't feel it.

Ilyas blurred to the desk, examining Deadpool with the intensity of a scientist studying a new fungus. "Whoa. Fourth-wall awareness. Chaotic neutral alignment. Excessive pouches. You're like if one of my clones got really into leatherworking and poor life choices."

"Flattery will get you everywhere, Pinkie." Deadpool turned to him. "You must be the local speedster. Cool hair. The braid says 'discipline,' but the crooked tooth says 'I fist-fought a sidewalk and the sidewalk won.' I respect it."

Ribb hopped over, her tongue extending to gently poke Deadpool's arm. She recoiled slightly. *"Ribbit. Tastes like... regret, cured meat, and copyright infringement."*

"Accurate!" Deadpool said. "Now, about my benefits package."

#### **Chapter 2: The "Interview"

Monika gestured to a chair that hadn't been there a second before. "Sit."

Deadpool sat. The chair squeaked obnoxiously. He bounced a little. "Squeaky chair. Classic power play. I'm into it."

"Your qualifications," Monika stated, her hands steepled.

"Okay, bullet points: Immortal, cancer-riddled, morally flexible, master assassin, expert in breaking the fourth wall, can regenerate from a single cell if the plot demands it, and I once took down an entire helicarrier with a spork and a bad attitude. Oh, and I'm **great** at parties."

"I see." Monika's smile remained. "Reason for seeking employment at this specific multiversal nexus?"

"Right, the classic 'why us' question. Smart." Deadpool leaned forward. "One: my home universe kind of... blew up. Totally not my fault. There was a thing with a unicorn, a vibranium cheesegrater, and a very sensitive deity. Long story. Need a new zip code."

"Understood."

"Two: I hear the health insurance is to die for. Literally, in my case, but then I'd get better and still be covered. That's a win-win."

"Plausible."

"Three: Pinkie over there—" he thumbed at Ilyas, "—apparently makes a chimichanga that can literally transcend dimensions. I need that in my life. I *crave* that in my life."

"And four?"

Deadpool spread his arms. "Look at this place! It's a narrative goldmine! So many cameos waiting to happen! I can be the guy who handles the messy stuff. The paradoxes you can't just file in triplicate. Someone creates a time loop because they married their own grandpa? I go in, create a *bigger, funnier* paradox that cancels it out. It's like homeopathy for reality! And!" He lowered his voice to a conspiratorial whisper. "I can be your secret weapon. The mysterious hero who saves the day, whose identity is a shocking secret..."

Ilyas raised an eyebrow. "You're wearing a bright red suit. Your face is a known variable."

"Ah! But that's the secret identity!" Deadpool waggled a finger. "By day, I am... **The Janitor.**"

Silence.

"You... you want to be the janitor?" Ilyas asked, slowly.

"Not *want* to be. I *will* be the janitor. But SECRETLY, I'm the superhero who saves the Hub from existential threats! But the REAL secret, the twist that'll make readers spit out their coffee, is that I'm ACTUALLY just the janitor! The superhero thing is just a cover! It's layered! It's meta! IT'S PEAK WRITING!" He was standing on the chair now.

Monika didn't blink. "Your proposed salary?"

"Exposure. And chimichangas. And a locker. With my name on it. In glitter."

Monika made a note in her ledger. "Very well. Provisional employment. Trial period. You will report to Ilyas for custodial duties and paradox containment. You will adhere to Hub conduct guidelines."

Deadpool pumped his fist. "Yes! When do I start?"

"You already have," Monika said. "There's a bio-hazardous spill in Portal Corridor 7-B. A gelatinous entity from a world of pure emotion got over-excited."

"On it!" Deadpool leapt from the chair, drawing his katanas. "I'll mop it up with the power of CLEANLINESS and VIOLENCE!"

As he sprinted off, Ilyas turned to Monika. "Moni-senpai. What the actual hell was that?"

"A variable," she said simply, her smile softening a micron. "An unpredictable one. The narrative entropy he brings might be useful. And," she added, "he's right about the health insurance. It's very comprehensive."

#### **Chapter 3: The Filter and The Footwear

An hour later, Deadpool was "cleaning." This involved using a plasma cannon to blast a stain off the ceiling while simultaneously arguing with Ilyas Clone #68 about mop bucket protocol.

**"—and another thing, you knock-off bargain bin Flash, if you don't let me use the industrial bleach on the eldritch horror in the breakroom, I'm gonna—"**

A soft, digital *bzzt* sound cut through the air.

Deadpool’s next word was garbled. It sounded like "What the cluck?" He tried again. "Son of a glitch!" He looked around, confused. "Hey, who censored my audio? This is a violation of my First Amendment rights, my creative integrity, and my brand!"

Monika’s voice came calmly over the newly installed intercom. "Hub Regulation 7.12: Excessive profanity and certain graphically descriptive threats can destabilize nearby reality-warping entities and upset the frog. A perceptual filter has been applied to your vocal output for all Hub-related communications. It will translate inappropriate language into contextually appropriate, less disruptive terms."

Deadpool was stunned. "You... you gave me a swear filter?"

Ilyas zipped by, grinning. "Welcome to the team, buddy! Now you're family-friendly! Well, family-*adjacent*. Like that weird uncle who talks about his 'collection' of katanas but mom says we have to invite him anyway."

Deadpool stood silently for a full minute. Then, a slow, maniacal grin spread under his mask. "Oh. Oh, this is *beautiful.* This is a challenge! I will make 'fluffernutter' sound like the most devastating insult in the multiverse! I will weaponize whimsy! THIS IS ART!"

From that day forward, Deadpool was a fixture. He "solved" a paradox by introducing a second, more handsome Deadpool from another timeline, causing the original paradox to die of jealousy. He "cleaned" the Complaint Department by "filing" every complaint directly into the sun (via a handy portal). He drove Monika to the edge of her calm smile by submitting his time sheets in the form of interpretive dance.

And true to his word, his greatest secret was that he was, in fact, just the janitor. When a panicked godling once burst into the Hub seeking "the mighty warrior who had slain the Chrono-Wyrm," only to be directed to a man in a red suit unclogging a toilet in the interdimensional restroom with a katana, the look on the godling’s face was, as Deadpool had promised, **PEAK**.

Ilyas found him leaning on his mop afterwards. "So, secret identity preserved?"

Deadpool sighed, a puff of pink smoke (he’d been spending time with Dracoon) curling from his mask’s seam. "It's a living. A weird, messy, deeply inappropriate living. Now, where are those chimichangas? I saved your weird, glitchy, two-faced buddies from a papercut paradox. That's gotta be worth extra guac."

He was chaos. He was meta. He was filtered. He was, somehow, part of the Hub. And as Monika noted in her ledger, his arrival correlated with a 300% increase in incident reports and a 400% increase in solved cases. She filed both under "Acceptable Anomalies."

The Hub now had its janitor. Its secret weapon. Its problem child. Its Merc.

It was never going to be boring again.
